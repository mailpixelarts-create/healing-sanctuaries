About the Healer

Sage Wildroot

For twenty-five years Sage has walked the forests, meadows, and riverbanks of the Pacific Northwest, learning the quiet language of plants. What began as a childhood spent pressing wildflowers between the pages of her grandmother's dictionaries has become a lifelong devotion to the art and science of herbal medicine. She forages each specimen by hand, guided by seasons and intuition, crafting tinctures, essences, and teas that honor the intelligence of every root, leaf, and petal. Her practice is not a clinic — it is a conversation between human and botanical worlds, a space where healing unfolds at the pace of growth.

Pressed Specimens

Each specimen below arrives as you scroll — a fragment of the living archive Sage has built over decades of fieldwork and study.

Echinacea purpurea

Purple Coneflower

Gathered at dawn along the Willamette trail in late July, when the purple petals are at their most vivid and the central cone glistens with morning dew. This specimen was pressed between pages of an old botany field guide and left to dry for three weeks. Echinacea has been Sage's most trusted ally for bolstering the immune system during the damp Portland winters — she steepes the dried root in warm water and honey, creating a remedy that tastes of earth and quiet strength. The plant teaches patience; its healing is slow but certain, building resilience one cup at a time.

Hypericum perforatum

St. John's Wort

Found growing wild on the sunlit banks of the Columbia River Gorge, where the summer light falls in long golden sheets across the hillside. Sage harvests the flowering tops in late June, hanging them upside down in her drying room until the petals crumble at a touch. The resulting oil glows a deep, almost translucent red — a color she calls "captured sunlight." This remedy is her go-to for emotional shadows, for the grey days when the spirit dims. A few drops beneath the tongue, and the world seems to find its color again. It is not a cure, she says, but a reminder that light always returns.

Valeriana officinalis

Valerian Root

Dug from the rich, dark soil of Sage's own garden bed after the first autumn rains, when the root system has stored its deepest potency. The smell is pungent — earthy, musky, almost animal — and not everyone appreciates it, but Sage considers it the scent of deep rest made manifest. She tinctures the fresh root in grain alcohol for six weeks, shaking the jar each morning as a small ritual of intention. The resulting extract is reserved for those nights when sleep will not come, when the mind races through corridors of worry. A dropperful in warm water, and the body remembers how to surrender. It is, she says, the plant's way of saying "you have done enough for today."

Calendula officinalis

Pot Marigold

Grown in terra cotta pots on the windowsill of Sage's kitchen, where they catch the morning light and turn their faces toward the sun with an almost devotional faithfulness. The petals are harvested individually, a meditative task she performs while listening to old radio programs. Infused into olive oil for several weeks, they produce a golden salve that heals cuts, burns, and dry skin with a gentleness that pharmaceutical creams cannot match. Sage calls calendula "the plant that teaches us to be soft without being weak" — its resilience lies in flexibility, in the willingness to bend with wind and weather without breaking.

Matricaria chamomilla

German Chamomile

A handful of dried chamomile flowers, their golden hearts still fragrant after months in a glass jar with a cork stopper. Sage sources these from a small organic farm in the Yamhill Valley, where the grower lets the plants go to seed each year so they return unbidden, wilder and more abundant than before. She brews them into a tea so delicate it tastes like sunlight filtered through honey — a remedy for anxious hearts and churning stomachs. She often gives it to children, watching their eyes grow heavy and peaceful as the warmth spreads through them. Chamomile is her reminder that medicine need not be bitter to be powerful, that healing can arrive disguised as a kindness.

Taraxacum officinale

Dandelion

The most misunderstood of all plants, Sage says — dismissed as a weed by those who do not know its gifts. She digs the roots from her own lawn each spring, when the bitter green leaves are still young and tender. The root, roasted slowly until it turns the color of dark coffee, makes a decoction that supports the liver and kidneys with quiet, persistent efficiency. It is the first remedy she reaches for when clients arrive with the heaviness of accumulated toxins, the sluggishness that comes from living too long indoors. Dandelion teaches that nothing is truly unwanted — that every plant, every creature, every moment has a purpose waiting to be recognized.

Sacred Art

The Practice

Three disciplines form the heart of Sage's work with plant medicine, each one a bridge between the wild intelligence of the botanical world and the human body's innate capacity for renewal.

01

Herbal Tinctures

Each tincture begins with a single plant, harvested at the precise moment of its peak vitality. Sage submerges the fresh material in grain alcohol and allows time — weeks, sometimes months — to draw out the plant's essence. The result is a concentrated liquid that carries the intelligence of the root or leaf in a form the body can readily absorb. She works with dozens of species, crafting bespoke formulations that address each client's particular needs with the specificity of a conversation rather than a prescription.

02

Flower Essences

Flower essences operate on the emotional and energetic body rather than the physical. Sage collects blossoms at sunrise, floating them in spring water exposed to sunlight until the water absorbs the flower's vibrational signature. This is then preserved in brandy and administered in drops beneath the tongue. She uses them to address anxiety, grief, fear, and the countless subtle disharmonies that undermine well-being before they manifest as physical symptoms. The essences are subtle but persistent, working like a tuning fork for the soul.

03

Medicinal Teas

The simplest and most ancient form of plant medicine, the tea is also the most intimate. Sage blends each blend by hand, combining dried herbs in proportions calibrated to the individual's constitution and the season's demands. She teaches her clients the art of slow preparation — measuring, steeping, waiting — as a practice of mindfulness. The act of making tea becomes a daily ritual of self-care, a small ceremony that anchors the body and calms the mind. Every blend tells a story of place, of the specific meadow or forest floor where each ingredient was gathered.

Philosophy

Plant Wisdom

I

The Living Pharmacy

Every forest, meadow, and roadside ditch is a dispensary waiting to be read. The plants that grow most abundantly around us are often the ones we need most — nature does not place medicine by accident. Learning to see the pharmacy that surrounds us is the first act of herbal mastery, and it requires nothing more than attention and willingness to kneel in the dirt.

II

Seasonal Attunement

The body, like the landscape, moves through cycles of growth, rest, and renewal. Sage teaches her clients to align their remedies and routines with the turning of the year — cleansing in spring, nourishing in summer, preserving in autumn, and resting in winter. This is not merely practical advice but a deeper invitation to remember that we are part of the same intelligence that moves the sap and calls the birds.

III

Root and Rhythm

Healing is not a destination but a rhythm — a pulse of effort and surrender, action and stillness. The plant world embodies this truth in every season. A seed does not rush to become a tree, and a wound does not heal by force of will alone. Sage guides her clients toward patience with the process, trust in the body's intelligence, and the understanding that the deepest healing often arrives in the quietest moments.

IV

Reciprocity with the Earth

To take from the earth is to enter into a covenant. Sage never harvests more than a tenth of any wild population, always leaves an offering of gratitude, and cultivates medicinal gardens to supplement what she gathers from the wild. This ethic of reciprocity is not sentiment but survival — the land that sustains us must itself be sustained, or the medicine dries up and the conversation ends.

Your Journey

What to Expect

Your first visit is not a consultation but a conversation — unhurried, exploratory, rooted in genuine curiosity about your body's story and the life you are living. Sage will ask questions that no doctor has asked, and together you will map the terrain of your well-being.

01

The Gathering

Before your visit, Sage will ask you to keep a journal — noting your sleep, your energy, your moods, and any symptoms that trouble you. She reads these notes the way a naturalist reads a trail: each entry a clue, each pattern a signal.

02

The Listening

Your first session lasts ninety minutes. Sage will take your history, ask about your relationship with food, with nature, with rest. She observes your pulse, your complexion, the quality of your voice. This is the heart of the practice — the moment where plant and person begin to find each other.

03

The Crafting

Within a week, Sage will have prepared your personalized tincture or tea blend. Each formula is made by hand in small batches, using plants she has gathered or grown herself. She includes detailed instructions and a letter explaining the reasoning behind each ingredient.

04

The Tending

Follow-up sessions occur monthly, where Sage adjusts your formula as the seasons change and your body responds. She teaches you to notice small shifts — a deeper breath, a calmer morning, a clearer mind — that indicate the medicine is finding its way.
