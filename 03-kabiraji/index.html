02

The Mechanism

Asana is not stretching. It is a conversation between your fascia, your nervous system, and the meridians that map your body's hidden highways.

Fascia Remembers

Your fascia is a continuous web of connective tissue that holds your body's shape and memory. When it hydrates and lengthens through mindful movement, it releases patterns of tension that muscles alone cannot reach. This is why a single pose held with awareness can shift what years of forced stretching cannot.

Meridians Map the Invisible

Traditional Chinese Medicine identified 12 meridian pathways long before modern anatomy mapped the fascial lines. They are the same highways. When you stretch your inner thigh in Butterfly Pose, you are not just opening the adductors — you are clearing the Liver meridian. The body is its own cartography.

The Nervous System Decides

Asana activates the parasympathetic nervous system through isometric contraction and breath synchronisation. Your heart rate variability increases. Your cortisol drops. Your body exits survival mode and enters its default state: restoration. You do not heal by force. You heal by permission.

06

Questions

I'm not flexible. Can I still do yoga?

Yoga is not a contortion contest. Flexibility is a byproduct, not a prerequisite. If you can breathe, you can do yoga. Maya designs every class with modifications for all body types, injuries, and experience levels. Your body's limitations are not obstacles — they are the starting point of the practice. The stiffest students often become the most devoted, because they feel the shift fastest.

Why is breathing more important than the pose?

Breath dictates heart rate variability — the single best predictor of resilience and recovery. A pose without breath is gymnastics. A pose with breath is medicine. When you synchronise movement with inhalation and exhalation, you activate the parasympathetic nervous system, reduce cortisol, and increase proprioceptive feedback to the cerebellum. The pose is the vessel. The breath is the medicine.

Can I injure myself?

If you feel a sharp, shooting, or electrical pain — stop immediately. That is your body's alarm system. A dull, broad sensation of stretch is normal and safe. A sharp point of pain is not. Maya teaches you to distinguish between the two. The greatest risk in yoga is not the pose — it is the ego that insists on going deeper before the body is ready. Listen to the sensation, not the instructor's count.

How is this different from a regular yoga class?

Most yoga classes follow a choreographed sequence — the teacher decides the flow. Maya's approach is fascia-led and meridian-mapped. She reads your body's tells — where you hold, where you compensate, where you avoid — and adjusts the sequence in real time. No two classes are identical. This is not a workout. It is a diagnostic conversation between your body and a teacher who knows how to listen.

What should I expect after my first session?

You may feel deeply relaxed, slightly lightheaded, or emotionally stirred. This is normal. Your nervous system has shifted from sympathetic (fight-or-flight) to parasympathetic (rest-and-digest). Some people feel a buzz of energy; others feel like they could sleep for hours. Both responses are valid. Hydrate well, avoid alcohol for 24 hours, and notice what shifts in the hours and days that follow. The practice continues after you leave the mat.

Do I need to follow a specific diet or lifestyle?

No. Maya does not prescribe dietary rules or lifestyle mandates. The practice is the practice. Some practitioners naturally gravitate towards lighter foods as their awareness deepens — not because they were told to, but because their body begins to ask for it. The yoga creates the awareness. The awareness creates the change. Force has no place here.
